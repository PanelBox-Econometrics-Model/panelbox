ZERO-INFLATION STRUCTURE:
=========================

Two groups of firms:
1. "Non-innovators" (structural zeros): ~20%
   - Low/zero R&D intensity
   - Traditional sectors
   - Older, established firms in stable markets
   - Never innovate during observation period

2. "Active innovators" (count process): ~80%
   - Positive R&D investment
   - May have zero innovations in some years (bad luck, development lag)
   - Innovations follow overdispersed count distribution

Implication: ZINB likely best model

OVERDISPERSION SOURCES:
=======================
1. Unobserved firm heterogeneity (capabilities, culture)
2. Project-level uncertainty (success/failure)
3. Lumpy innovation (multiple innovations cluster in time)
4. Strategic timing (patent clusters)
5. Measurement issues (reporting variations)

PANEL STRUCTURE CONSIDERATIONS:
================================

Within variation (65% of total):
- R&D changes over time
- Policy interventions (public funding)
- Market conditions evolve
- Allows Fixed Effects estimation

Between variation (35% of total):
- Tech sector differences
- Size/scale effects
- VC backing
- Allows Random Effects estimation

Hausman test expected to reject RE → use FE

KEY EMPIRICAL PATTERNS:
=======================

R&D effects:
- R&D intensity elasticity: 0.85 (10% more R&D → 8.5% more innovations)
- R&D personnel: 1% more per additional researcher
- Diminishing returns at very high R&D levels

Firm characteristics:
- Size elasticity: 0.15 (larger firms innovate more, but less than proportionally)
- Age effect: Inverted U-shape (peak at 15 years)
- Tech sector premium: 65% more innovations

Policy variables:
- Public funding: +30% innovations (AME ≈ 1.35 innovations)
- University collaboration: +45% innovations
- Public funding × university interaction: +15% additional

Market structure:
- Competition effect: Non-monotonic (peak at moderate competition)
- Very high competition discourages risky innovation
- Monopoly/low competition reduces innovation incentives

Knowledge spillovers:
- Alliance count: +12% per additional alliance
- Export share: +0.8% per percentage point (learning from export markets)
- Intellectual property stock: Weak effect (appropriability vs disclosure trade-off)

IDENTIFICATION STRATEGY:
========================

Challenges:
1. R&D endogeneity: Innovative firms invest more in R&D (reverse causality)
2. Selection: Subsidies go to promising firms (selection bias)
3. Unobserved heterogeneity: Firm capabilities correlate with R&D

Solutions employed:
1. Panel Fixed Effects: Control for time-invariant heterogeneity
2. Lagged R&D: Use t-1 R&D to reduce simultaneity
3. Matching: Compare subsidy recipients to similar non-recipients
4. Exclusion restrictions: Distance to university affects collab, not innovation directly

Preferred specification:
- ZINB with firm fixed effects (computationally intensive)
- Lagged R&D variables
- Control for time trends
- Cluster standard errors by firm

HETEROGENEOUS TREATMENT EFFECTS:
=================================

Public funding effect varies by:

Small firms (revenue < 50M):
  - AME: +1.8 innovations (+55%)
  - More binding financial constraints
  - Higher marginal value of subsidy

Large firms (revenue > 200M):
  - AME: +0.9 innovations (+18%)
  - Less financially constrained
  - Subsidy is smaller relative to own R&D

Young firms (age < 10):
  - AME: +2.1 innovations (+70%)
  - Building capabilities
  - Subsidy enables risk-taking

Mature firms (age > 25):
  - AME: +0.7 innovations (+15%)
  - Established routines
  - Less responsive to incentives

Implication: Target subsidies to small, young firms for maximum impact

POLICY IMPLICATIONS:
====================

1. R&D subsidies are effective:
   - Additionality: Public funding increases innovation
   - No evidence of crowding out private R&D
   - Larger effects for constrained firms

2. University-industry collaboration:
   - Strong positive effect on innovation
   - Policy should facilitate partnerships
   - Geographic proximity matters (regional innovation systems)

3. Competition policy:
   - Moderate competition optimal for innovation
   - Very high competition may discourage risky R&D
   - Merger review should consider innovation effects

4. Intellectual property:
   - Patents weakly related to innovations (disclosure costs?)
   - Trade secrets may be preferred for some innovations
   - IP policy effects are nuanced

ROBUSTNESS CHECKS PERFORMED:
=============================

1. Alternative dependent variable:
   - Patents instead of innovations
   - Results qualitatively similar
   - R&D elasticity slightly higher for patents (0.95 vs 0.85)

2. Sample restrictions:
   - Exclude smallest firms (<50 employees): Results strengthen
   - Exclude largest firms (>1000 employees): Results similar
   - Tech sector only: R&D effects larger

3. Time periods:
   - Pre-COVID (2015-2019): R&D effects stronger
   - COVID period (2020-2022): Innovations fell 15%, R&D-innovation link weaker

4. Outliers:
   - Winsorize top 1% of innovations: Slight attenuation of effects
   - Exclude firms with >20 innovations/year: Results robust

5. Functional form:
   - Hurdle model vs ZINB: Very similar results
   - Poisson vs ZINB: Poisson severely underestimates SEs
   - Linear model: Predicts negative innovations for some firms (invalid)

PRACTICAL INSIGHTS FOR RESEARCHERS:
====================================

1. Always start with data exploration:
   - Check zero proportion
   - Plot variance vs mean
   - Examine time trends
   - Descriptive stats by groups

2. Model selection is crucial:
   - Don't default to Poisson
   - Test for overdispersion
   - Check for zero-inflation
   - Use information criteria

3. Report multiple specifications:
   - Baseline model
   - Preferred model
   - Robustness checks
   - Show sensitivity to assumptions

4. Marginal effects, not coefficients:
   - Coefficients are hard to interpret
   - Marginal effects in outcome units
   - Show heterogeneity

5. Visualize results:
   - Predicted probabilities
   - Marginal effects plots
   - Rootograms
   - Coefficient plots with CIs
